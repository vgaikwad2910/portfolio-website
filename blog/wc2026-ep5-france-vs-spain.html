France vs Spain: I Found a Bug in My Model — and Five Signals Agreed Anyway

Vaishnavi Gaikwad · July 9, 2026 · 11 min read · GitHub

The last episode did not go well. My model had Norway as the 55.6% favorite over England in the quarterfinal. England won 2–1. I got it wrong.

So before doing anything else today, I went back into the code. And I found something embarrassing: the defense term in my lambda formula was inverted. For four episodes, my model had been treating a stronger defense as a bonus for the opposing team's attack. It was backwards.

After fixing that — and adding five new features, a confirmed lineup, and prediction market signals — here is what the data says about France vs Spain.

The Bug: Inverted Defense

The Bayesian Poisson model estimates the expected goals for each team as a function of that team's attack strength and the opponent's defense strength. Here is the original formula I was using:

# WRONG — defense was inverted
λ_A = (atk_A / baseline) × (baseline / def_B) × quality × baseline

The problem: when a team has a strong defense (low xGA → def_B < baseline), the term baseline / def_B becomes greater than 1. Better defense → higher opponent expected goals. That is exactly the opposite of what it should do.

# CORRECT — defense properly suppresses opponent attack
λ_A = (atk_A / baseline) × (def_B / baseline) × quality × baseline

# Simplifies to: atk_A × def_B × quality / baseline
# When def_B is strong (low xGA → def_B/baseline < 1), λ_A is suppressed ✓

Retroactive validation: running Norway vs England with the corrected formula gives England 63.2%. England won. The fixed model had it right; the broken one didn't.

Why this mattered most for that specific matchup: Norway had a game where they scored 2 on just 1.05 xGF (massive overperformance vs Brazil). England's xGA included a blowout outlier. The inverted formula amplified both errors simultaneously — it saw Norway's poor underlying defense as a boost for Norway's attack, and England's strong defense as a gift to Norway's lambda. Two compounding mistakes, same direction.

Three Structural Fixes

Fix 1: Winsorize xG at 2.5

Single outlier games (Norway's 1.05 xGF blowout, France's 3.24 xGF against a weak group-stage opponent) were pulling the model too hard. Any xGF or xGA above 2.5 per game gets capped at 2.5 before averaging. It's a standard robustness technique — you still see that the game was strong, but you don't let one outlier dominate six games of signal.

Fix 2: Opponent quality scaling

A 3.24 xGF against a group-stage side is not the same as a 3.24 xGF against a quarterfinalist. Every game in the sample now gets quality-adjusted before entering the model:

RoundQuality factorEffect on xGFEffect on xGA
Group stage0.72× 0.72 (deflated)÷ 0.72 (inflated)

Scoring against a weaker opponent gets down-weighted; conceding against a weaker opponent gets up-weighted. This substantially reduces France's raw dominance numbers while barely touching their QF data — the most tournament-relevant games now carry the most weight.

Fix 3: Stronger Bayesian prior at the semifinal stage

At the quarterfinal, I used a prior weight of 3 imaginary "average" games. For the semifinal, I increased that to 4. With only 6 games of data, we are still working with a small sample. Stronger shrinkage toward the tournament baseline (1.35 xG/game) prevents two or three exceptional performances from dominating the posterior.

Going Deeper: Five New Features

For this episode I added five features the base Poisson model doesn't capture. Each one has empirical grounding; none of them are arbitrary.

Feature 1: Confirmed Lineup — FC 26 Player Composites

Using confirmed starting XIs (not predicted), I computed attacking and defensive composite scores from FC 26 player ratings. Attack composite = average of Shooting + Dribbling divided by 2. Defense composite = average DEF rating. Both normalized against a baseline of 72.

France starts Barcola over Doué — a meaningful difference (Doué rates slightly higher in FC 26 at 79/83 vs Barcola's 77/80). France's ATK edge comes primarily from Mbappé (94/92 SHO/DRI), the highest-rated active player at this tournament. Quality multipliers: France generating chances against Spain's defense → 1.019×. Spain generating chances against France's defense → 0.993×. A near-wash — these two teams are structurally matched at an elite level.

Feature 2: Finishing Efficiency (50% regressed)

Goals scored versus expected goals for the tournament:

  • France: 16 goals on 14.3 xGF → 1.12× conversion. Regressed 50% toward 1.0 → +5.9% multiplier on France's λ
  • Spain: 11 goals on 12.1 xGF → 0.91× conversion. Regressed 50% toward 1.0 → −4.4% multiplier on Spain's λ

Full regression would assume all finishing is luck. Full credit would assume it's all skill. 50% is the pragmatic middle: Mbappé's 26.7% conversion rate on 30 shots across the tournament isn't luck.

Feature 3: Shot Quality (xG per shot on target)

Not all xG is created equal. France's average xG per shot on target is 0.304 vs the World Cup average of 0.22. That's not just volume — it's shot selection. Closer to goal, better angles, less defensive pressure when pulling the trigger. This adds a +5.7% multiplier to France's attack. Spain at 0.26 xG/SOT adds a +2.7%.

Feature 4: Fatigue Adjustment

France's quarterfinal was played July 9. Spain's was July 10. France had one extra rest day heading into the semifinal. At elite level, the marginal effect is small but measurable — estimated at roughly 1.5% per extra rest day. France: +1.5%. Spain: no adjustment.

Feature 5: Form Momentum

Comparing each team's quality-adjusted xGF across the last three versus first three games of the tournament:

  • France: first 3 games avg 1.50 adj. xGF/game → last 3 games avg 1.90. Trend: improving. +2.7%
  • Spain: first 3 games avg 1.36 → last 3 games avg 1.83. Also improving. +3.5%

Momentum sensitivity is set at 10% — enough to register a meaningful peak without overclaiming a linear trend across only 6 games.

Kalshi gets the highest weight (30%) because it is CFTC-regulated, has no house edge baked into the margin, and had $8.3M in trading volume on this match — large enough to represent real information. The range across all five sources is 5.2 percentage points (54.0% to 59.2%), with a standard deviation of 1.9pp. When five completely independent methods land in a tight window, the consensus is meaningful. This isn't noise canceling out — it's convergence.

The Mbappé Factor

One thing the model captures indirectly through finishing efficiency and shot quality, but which is worth stating explicitly: Kylian Mbappé has been the best individual performer at this World Cup. Eight goals. Three assists. Thirty shots across six games. 26.7% conversion rate — not just volume, but efficiency.

In FC 26 terms, Mbappé rates 94 SHO / 92 DRI — the highest attacking composite of any active player at the tournament. The model uses this through the player composite multiplier, but it doesn't capture the psychological weight of a player opponents must double-mark even when out of possession. That's a real edge the numbers understate.

Spain's answer is structural: Rodri anchoring the midfield at 88 DEF, reducing the spaces Mbappé typically exploits in transition. The tactical duel between Mbappé on the break and Rodri cutting passing lanes might decide this match more than any other individual factor.

What the model still can't see:

  • Tactical shape — Spain's 4-3-3 is designed to suffocate vertical transitions, which is exactly how France generate their best xG (Mbappé running in behind)
  • Yamal on the right side against Digne — a significant pace mismatch the Poisson model ignores entirely
  • Set piece quality — both teams are elite from dead balls, and the model treats every goal attempt equally regardless of delivery type
  • Manager decisions: Deschamps is conservative late in matches; Luis Enrique's Spain is positionally aggressive. Tactical intent diverges when matches are level
  • Penalty shootout variance — even with France at 58% in pens, a five-kick shootout is still close to a coin flip in any single realization

France 57.8%. Spain 42.2%. My model says France — but this is not a prediction I'm confident in the way I'd want to be. The signal convergence is real; the uncertainty is also real. Either team winning would not surprise any honest observer of this tournament.

What I know now that I didn't know in Episode 1: the numbers are honest about what they don't know. A 57.8% probability isn't a prediction that France wins. It's a statement that if this match were played 100 times under identical conditions, France would probably win about 58 of them. The other 42 go to Spain. Watch the match like someone who's read both sides of that sentence.
