LinkedIn to Stanford: How I've Approached Learning as a Working Professional

April 2026 · 6 min read · Vaishnavi Gaikwad

This winter I completed three courses at Stanford Continuing Studies — on statistical foundations, data visualization, and large language models. It's a long way from where my self-directed learning journey started, which was largely LinkedIn Learning articles and YouTube tutorials squeezed between work calls.

I've tried most of the major learning platforms over the past several years. Here's an honest account of what each offered, what it didn't, and what I actually think about how to learn this stuff as a working professional.

The Platforms, Honestly

LinkedIn Learning
Best for: orientation and surface-level exposure

Good for getting a working vocabulary and understanding what a field involves. Not deep enough to actually build skills. I used it most in the very early stages when I was trying to understand what I didn't know yet — which turns out to be a valuable thing to figure out.

Udemy
Best for: specific technical skills, fast

Wildly inconsistent quality, but the good courses are genuinely good. Practical, project-based, affordable. I used Udemy to fill specific gaps — Python fundamentals, SQL, particular ML algorithms — rather than for structured learning paths. Worth it at sale prices.

DataCamp
Best for: building hands-on muscle memory

The interactive coding environment is genuinely useful for building fluency. I credit DataCamp more than anything else for getting me to the point where Python stopped feeling foreign. The content is solid for analytics and data science, though it can feel shallow once you've moved past intermediate level.

Coursera
Best for: structured, rigorous curricula

University-backed courses with real academic rigor. Andrew Ng's Machine Learning Specialization was the most structured introduction to ML concepts I found. Takes real commitment — this isn't background-tab content. I got the most out of Coursera when I treated the courses like an actual class with deadlines I held myself to.

Stanford Continuing Studies
Best for: theoretical depth + practitioner perspective

A different category from everything else on this list. Courses taught by practitioners with real accountability — live sessions, instructor interaction, cohort learning. This winter I completed three courses: Statistical Foundations for AI/ML/Data Science, Data Visualization with Python, and LLMs for Business. The depth and the quality of teaching were noticeably different from self-paced alternatives.

What I Actually Think About Self-Directed Learning

No amount of learning ever finishes. I used to find that demoralizing. Now I find it clarifying.

There's a specific trap in self-directed learning that I fell into for too long: treating learning as a thing you finish. If I just complete this specialization, I'll know enough. If I get this certificate, I'll be ready. That framing sets you up to feel perpetually behind, because the field keeps moving and the certificates keep multiplying.

The shift that helped me was treating learning as ongoing maintenance rather than a destination. The question isn't "when will I be done?" It's "what do I need to understand better to do my current work well, and what should I invest in now to be prepared for what comes next?"

What Actually Determines Whether Learning Sticks

Platform matters less than application speed. The fastest way to internalize something is to use it on a real problem within days of learning it — not weeks. When I took a course on causal inference and immediately applied the concepts to an experiment I was running at work, it stuck. When I took a course and filed it away to "use later," it mostly evaporated.

This is why building side projects alongside formal learning matters. The Netflix recommendation engine, the A/B testing simulator, the GenAI transcript analyzer I built this year — those projects forced application of concepts I was learning in real time. The learning from building those things outlasted any course content.

Advice for Working Professionals

Protect the time aggressively. Learning as a working professional competes with everything — work, family, rest. It doesn't happen without deliberate scheduling. I treat learning blocks like meetings I can't cancel.

Choose depth over breadth. It's tempting to collect certificates across many areas. One concept understood deeply is worth more than five concepts understood shallowly. Pick the thing most relevant to your work right now and go as deep as you can on it.

Don't wait until you feel ready to build. Build first. The gaps you discover while building will tell you what to learn next better than any curriculum.

LinkedIn to Stanford took several years. The honest answer is that the learning didn't get easier — I just got more comfortable with it being hard. That comfort is itself a skill worth building.
