I Don't Watch Football. So I Used Data Science to Fix That.

Vaishnavi Gaikwad · June 2026 · 8 min read · GitHub

I have a confession.

Everyone around me has been talking about the World Cup for weeks. Colleagues debating group stages, friends sending voice notes at midnight after matches, my entire LinkedIn feed suddenly full of football opinions.

And I've been nodding along, pretending I understand what's happening.

So I did what any data scientist would do. Instead of watching 90 minutes of a sport I don't follow, I spent a weekend building a tool to explain it to me through data.

Here's what I built, what I found, and — more importantly — where the data falls short.

What I Built

The project has four main components.

A data pipeline. I used EA Sports FC 26 ratings, which cover 18,406 players across every professional club and national team in the world. These ratings cover six core attributes per player: pace (PAC), shooting (SHO), passing (PAS), dribbling (DRI), defending (DEF), and physicality (PHY).

A position-weighted composite scorer. This is the core idea. A goalkeeper and a striker shouldn't be evaluated on the same stats. A keeper's DEF and PHY matter far more than their SHO. A striker's SHO and PAC matter more than their DEF. So each player gets a composite score weighted by what their position actually demands:

Position Key Weights
GKDEF 45%, PHY 35%, PAS 8%
CBDEF 47%, PHY 25%, PAC 10%
CMPAS 28%, DRI 18%, SHO 13%
STSHO 37%, PAC 20%, DRI 20%

A squad ranking engine. For each national team, I take the top 23 players by composite score and average them. This gives a single "squad strength" number per country.

A Monte Carlo match predictor. Given two squads, I model each team's attack strength vs. the opponent's defense strength, convert that into an expected goals rate (λ), and simulate 10,000 matches using a Poisson distribution. The result is a win/draw/loss probability for any head-to-head matchup.

The full code — data pipeline, scorer, FAISS similarity engine, and Streamlit app — is open source on GitHub.

France at #1 makes intuitive sense — they have Mbappé, a deep midfield, and arguably the strongest defensive unit in the tournament. Brazil at #2 reflects the sheer quality of their attacking depth. England at #3, despite often underperforming their squad strength, have the raw talent to justify it.

The Interesting Part: Argentina at #7

The defending world champions rank seventh by squad strength.

This isn't a knock on Argentina — it's actually what makes this kind of analysis interesting. Argentina won the World Cup in 2022 with a squad that, on paper, wasn't the strongest. They won on tournament experience, defensive organisation, Messi's individual brilliance at precisely the right moments, and — if you ask a Frenchman — some penalty lottery luck.

The model doesn't know any of that. It knows that Enzo Fernández's passing composite is 82.3, that Julián Álvarez scores 79.1 as a striker, and that their defensive line is solid but not exceptional. It doesn't know about Messi's presence in the dressing room or Argentina's penalty record.

That gap — between what the raw talent says and what actually happens on the pitch — is the most honest thing this model can tell you.

A few things stand out. The probabilities are genuinely close because at the top international level, squad quality differences are small — France and England are separated by just 1 point in composite score. The model gives France a 5% edge over England, not a landslide.

Morocco at 32.5% against Germany isn't a prediction that Morocco will win — it's a prediction that this is a genuinely competitive matchup. That's more interesting than dismissing them outright.

What This Model Can't See

I want to be honest about the limitations, because they're significant.

What it misses:

  • Current form (a player rated 85 who hasn't played in 3 weeks vs. one on a 10-game winning streak)
  • Tactical matchups (high press vs. low block, 4-3-3 vs. 5-4-1)
  • Injuries and suspensions during the tournament
  • Head-to-head history and psychological factors
  • Set piece quality (corners, free kicks are disproportionately impactful in international football)
  • The goalkeeper factor (a single elite goalkeeper can swing a tournament — Unai Simón in 2021, Emiliano Martínez in 2022)

What it's actually good for:

  • Establishing a baseline of squad quality before the tournament
  • Identifying which nations are "punching above their weight" (Argentina, Croatia historically) or "underperforming their talent" (Belgium, Portugal)
  • Giving me, someone who doesn't follow football, a principled way to understand why certain teams are taken seriously
